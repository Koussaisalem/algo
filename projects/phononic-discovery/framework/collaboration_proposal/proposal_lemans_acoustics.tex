\begin{abstract}
\noindent We propose a collaborative research program to discover novel phononic materials exhibiting relativistic quasiparticle dynamics using AI-driven quantum materials discovery. By leveraging recent advances in generative machine learning and density functional theory, we aim to identify crystalline systems where acoustic phonons experience effective spacetime curvature—creating the first laboratory analog of gravitational physics at the quantum scale. This work bridges condensed matter physics and general relativity, offering unprecedented opportunities to experimentally probe the interface between quantum mechanics and gravity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Scientific Motivation: The Quantum-Relativity Frontier}

The marriage of quantum mechanics and general relativity remains one of physics' greatest unsolved challenges. While both theories are extraordinarily successful in their respective domains, their unification—a quantum theory of gravity—has eluded researchers for nearly a century.

\subsection{The Analog Gravity Paradigm}

Rather than attempting to quantize gravity directly, \textbf{analog gravity} offers an experimentally accessible alternative: engineer systems where quantum excitations propagate through \textit{effective} curved spacetime geometries~\cite{barcelo2011analogue}. The key insight is mathematical equivalence:

\begin{keybox}{Core Principle}
If a quasiparticle's equation of motion is mathematically identical to a massless relativistic particle in curved spacetime, the system provides a physical simulator for gravitational phenomena—regardless of the underlying microscopic physics.
\end{keybox}

This approach has already yielded remarkable successes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Acoustic black holes} in flowing Bose-Einstein condensates, where phonons experience event horizons and exhibit analog Hawking radiation~\cite{steinhauer2016observation}
    \item \textbf{Gravitational lensing} of electrons in strained graphene, where spatial variations in the Fermi velocity mimic gravitational potentials~\cite{guinea2010energy}
    \item \textbf{Cosmological expansion} simulations using cold atoms in time-dependent optical lattices~\cite{jain2007quantum}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Why Phononic Materials?}

Acoustic phonons—quantized lattice vibrations—represent ideal candidates for analog gravity experiments:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Natural Wave Phenomena}: Phonons are intrinsically wavelike, making them sensitive to effective spacetime geometry
    \item \textbf{Experimental Accessibility}: Phonon dispersion $\omega(\mathbf{k})$ is measurable via inelastic X-ray/neutron scattering, Raman spectroscopy, and Brillouin scattering
    \item \textbf{Tunability}: Strain, pressure, and temperature provide external knobs to modulate phononic properties
    \item \textbf{Room Temperature Operation}: Unlike cold atom systems, solid-state analogs function at practical temperatures
\end{enumerate}

\begin{impactbox}
\textbf{The Gap}: Despite these advantages, \textit{no systematic method exists} to discover materials with the specific phononic band structures required for analog gravity experiments. This is where AI-driven materials discovery becomes transformative.
\end{impactbox}

\section{Theoretical Framework: From Atoms to Curved Spacetime}

\subsection{Step 1: Dirac/Weyl Phononic Dispersion}

The journey from crystal lattice to emergent relativity begins with topology. In certain materials, phononic bands touch at discrete points in the Brillouin zone, forming conical intersections. Near these \textbf{Dirac} or \textbf{Weyl points}, the dispersion relation linearizes:

\begin{equation}
\omega(\mathbf{k}) \approx v_F |\mathbf{k} - \mathbf{k}_D|
\label{eq:dirac_dispersion}
\end{equation}

where $v_F$ is the characteristic velocity (analog of speed of light) and $\mathbf{k}_D$ is the Dirac point location. This is mathematically \textit{identical} to the energy-momentum relation for massless relativistic particles:

\begin{equation}
E = pc \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad \hbar\omega = \hbar v_F k
\end{equation}

\textbf{Physical Interpretation}: Phonons near the Dirac point behave as if they are photons propagating at speed $v_F$ instead of $c$.

\subsection{Step 2: Spatial Modulation Creates Curvature}

A constant $v_F$ corresponds to flat Minkowski spacetime. The breakthrough occurs when $v_F$ varies spatially—$v_F = v_F(\mathbf{r})$—which can be induced by:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Non-uniform strain fields $\epsilon_{ij}(\mathbf{r})$
    \item Compositional gradients in alloys
    \item Temperature gradients $T(\mathbf{r})$
\end{itemize}

When $v_F$ is position-dependent, the phonon's wave equation becomes:

\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{\sqrt{-g}} \partial_\mu \left( \sqrt{-g} \, g^{\mu\nu} \partial_\nu \right) \Phi = 0
\label{eq:curved_wave}
\end{equation}

where $g^{\mu\nu}(\mathbf{r})$ is an \textbf{effective spacetime metric} determined by the material's local properties~\cite{volovik2003universe}. The phonon propagates along geodesics of this emergent geometry—experiencing what appears as a gravitational field.

\subsection{Step 3: Engineering Exotic Geometries}

The metric tensor $g^{\mu\nu}$ encodes the curvature. For a phononic material with anisotropic velocity $v_i(\mathbf{r})$, the effective metric takes the form:

\begin{equation}
ds^2 = -\left( \frac{c^2}{v_F^2} - \frac{v_i v_i}{v_F^2} \right) dt^2 - 2\frac{v_i}{v_F^2} dx^i dt + \frac{1}{v_F^2} dx^i dx^i
\end{equation}

\begin{keybox}{Critical Insight}
By designing materials where $v_F(\mathbf{r})$ exhibits specific profiles, we can engineer:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gravitational wells}: Regions where phonons are "trapped" (slow $v_F$)
    \item \textbf{Lensing}: Phonon trajectories bend around strain concentrations
    \item \textbf{Horizons}: Boundaries where $v_F$ drops to zero (acoustic analogs of black holes)
    \item \textbf{Repulsive regions}: Materials where phonons are "repelled" (negative curvature)
\end{itemize}
\end{keybox}

The last point is particularly profound. Materials with \textit{anomalous} strain response—where compression increases phonon velocity rather than decreasing it—would exhibit effective \textbf{negative energy density}, mimicking the exotic matter required for repulsive gravity in general relativity.

\section{The Discovery Challenge: Why AI is Essential}

\subsection{The Computational Bottleneck}

Finding materials with the required phononic topology is a monumental challenge:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Vast Search Space}: $\sim10^{180}$ possible crystal structures (combinatorial explosion of elements, compositions, symmetries)
    \item \textbf{Expensive Calculations}: Accurate phonon band structures require Density Functional Perturbation Theory (DFPT), costing 10-100 CPU-hours per structure
    \item \textbf{Rare Target}: Dirac/Weyl points are topologically protected but statistically uncommon ($<$1\% of materials)
    \item \textbf{Multi-Objective Optimization}: Simultaneously require:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Linear phonon dispersion (Eq.~\ref{eq:dirac_dispersion})
        \item Thermodynamic stability ($\Delta H_f < 0$)
        \item Dynamic stability (no imaginary phonon modes)
        \item Synthesizability (compatible with MBE, CVD, or bulk methods)
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Estimate}: Screening $10^6$ candidates via brute-force DFPT would require $\sim$10 million CPU-hours—$\approx$1000 years on a single workstation.

\subsection{The AI Solution: Generative + Predictive Models}

Our approach combines two AI paradigms:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Component} & \textbf{Technology} & \textbf{Role} \\ \midrule
\textit{Generator} & Diffusion Models & Propose novel crystal structures \\
\textit{Predictor} & Graph Neural Networks & Fast phonon spectrum prediction \\
\textit{Optimizer} & Differentiable Objectives & Guide search toward Dirac topology \\ \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{AI pipeline components for topological phonon discovery.}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{Generative Models for Crystal Structures}

We employ \textbf{conditional diffusion models} trained on databases of stable crystals (Materials Project, OQMD). These models learn the statistical patterns of real materials—chemical bonding rules, symmetry constraints, coordination preferences—and generate \textit{chemically plausible} candidates rather than random atomic configurations.

\textbf{Key Advantage}: Unlike evolutionary algorithms or random sampling, diffusion models naturally explore high-probability regions of structure space while avoiding unphysical configurations.

\subsubsection{Equivariant Graph Neural Networks for Phonons}

Calculating phonon dispersions for millions of candidates via DFPT is impossible. Instead, we train \textbf{E(3)-equivariant Graph Neural Networks} (GNNs) to predict $\omega(\mathbf{k})$ directly from atomic positions~\cite{batzner2022mace}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Input}: Crystal structure (atomic coordinates, species, lattice vectors)
    \item \textbf{Output}: Phonon band structure along high-symmetry paths
    \item \textbf{Training}: $\sim$5000 DFPT calculations on diverse materials
    \item \textbf{Inference Speed}: $<$1 second per structure (10$^6\times$ faster than DFPT)
    \item \textbf{Accuracy}: 1-3\% mean absolute error on phonon frequencies
\end{itemize}

The \textit{equivariance} property ensures predictions respect physical symmetries—rotations, translations, and permutations of identical atoms—which is essential for capturing topological features like Dirac cones.

\subsubsection{Differentiable Topology Objectives}

The final component is a \textbf{loss function} that quantifies "Dirac cone quality":

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{Dirac}} = \underbrace{\lambda_1 \cdot \Delta\omega^2|_{\mathbf{k}_D}}_{\text{degeneracy}} + \underbrace{\lambda_2 \cdot (1 - R^2_{\text{linear}})}_{\text{linearity}} + \underbrace{\lambda_3 \cdot \sigma^2_{\text{azimuthal}}}_{\text{isotropy}}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta\omega|_{\mathbf{k}_D}$ is the band gap at the target k-point, $R^2_{\text{linear}}$ measures fit quality to Eq.~\ref{eq:dirac_dispersion}, and $\sigma^2_{\text{azimuthal}}$ quantifies rotational symmetry.

Because the GNN predictor is differentiable, gradients of $\mathcal{L}_{\text{Dirac}}$ flow back through the network to the atomic positions, enabling \textit{gradient-based optimization} toward topological targets—a fundamentally different paradigm than traditional materials discovery.

\section{Proof of Feasibility: Prior Work and Validated Components}

This proposal builds on established successes in both analog gravity and AI materials discovery:

\subsection{Analog Gravity: Experimentally Demonstrated}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Acoustic Black Holes}: Steinhauer (2016) measured spontaneous Hawking radiation from a sonic horizon in a BEC—confirming 40-year-old theoretical predictions~\cite{steinhauer2016observation}
    \item \textbf{Strained Graphene}: Guinea et al. (2010) showed strain-induced pseudomagnetic fields $>$300 Tesla for electrons, with subsequent experimental verification via STM~\cite{guinea2010energy,levy2010strain}
    \item \textbf{Topological Phonons}: Recent discoveries of Weyl phonons in materials like FeSi and NbAs demonstrate that topological band crossings exist in real crystals~\cite{li2018weyl}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Conclusion}: The physics is proven. The bottleneck is \textit{material discovery}.

\subsection{AI Materials Discovery: Validated Track Record}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{GNoME (Google DeepMind, 2023)}: Discovered 2.2 million novel stable crystals using GNN energy predictors—380,000 subsequently confirmed stable by independent DFT~\cite{merchant2023scaling}
    \item \textbf{CDVAE (2022)}: Diffusion model for crystal generation achieved 61\% validity on novel materials, with top candidates synthesized in lab~\cite{xie2021crystal}
    \item \textbf{NequIP/MACE}: Equivariant GNNs achieve DFT-level accuracy (1-3 meV/atom) for energy/force prediction, enabling million-structure screening campaigns~\cite{batzner2022mace}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Our Innovation}: We adapt these proven tools from \textit{energy optimization} to \textit{topology optimization}—replacing "find the lowest energy structure" with "find the most topologically interesting phonon spectrum."

\subsection{Current Infrastructure: Validated Components}

Our team has already developed and validated key components:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Generative Engine}: Operational diffusion model generating chemically valid crystal structures with $>$80\% stability rate (formation energy $<$0.2 eV/atom)
    \item \textbf{Surrogate Model}: Trained equivariant GNN predicting formation energies with 42 meV/atom MAE on QM9 dataset
    \item \textbf{DFT Validation Pipeline}: Automated workflow (GPAW) for structure relaxation, phonon calculations, and stability screening
    \item \textbf{Discovery Success}: Recently identified novel 2D CrCuSe$_2$ phase—a metastable hetero-metallic TMD with 0.6 eV bandgap and zero imaginary phonon modes, validated by independent computational chemistry expert
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{What's Required}: Extension of the surrogate model from energy to phonon prediction, and implementation of topology-aware loss functions. The infrastructure exists; the adaptation is straightforward.

    \item \textbf{Acoustic Analog Devices}: Decades of experience designing phononic waveguides, lenses, and topological insulators
    \item \textbf{Theoretical Acoustics}: Deep expertise in wave propagation, effective medium theory, and phononic band structure interpretation
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Our Contribution: Computational Discovery Engine}

We provide:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Material Candidates}: AI-generated crystal structures optimized for Dirac/Weyl phononic topology
    \item \textbf{DFT Predictions}: High-accuracy phonon band structures, elastic constants, and strain response tensors
    \item \textbf{Strain Engineering Protocols}: Computational design of non-uniform strain fields to create effective curvature
    \item \textbf{Synthesis Guidance}: Formation energy calculations, phase stability analysis, and growth parameter recommendations (MBE, CVD)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Collaborative Workflow}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Phase I (Months 1-6)}: \textit{Discovery Campaign}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Deploy AI engine to generate 1000 candidate materials with Dirac phonon cones
        \item Down-select top 50 via DFT validation (stability, synthesizability)
        \item Deliver ranked list with complete phonon dispersions and elastic properties
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Phase II (Months 6-12)}: \textit{Strain Engineering}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Design strain protocols for top 10 candidates to achieve $\nabla v_F \neq 0$
        \item Predict effective metric tensors and phonon trajectories in strained samples
        \item LAUM: Experimental validation via strain-dependent Brillouin measurements
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Phase III (Months 12-18)}: \textit{Analog Gravity Experiments}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item LAUM: Fabricate phononic samples with engineered strain profiles
        \item Measure phonon propagation in "curved spacetime" (lensing, deflection, trapping)
        \item Compare experimental data to GR predictions (geodesic equations)
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Phase IV (Months 18-24)}: \textit{Exotic Regimes}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Search for materials with anomalous strain response (repulsive analog)
        \item Design and test "negative energy density" phononic systems
        \item Publication: First experimental realization of repulsive gravity analog
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Mature AI Tools}: Generative models and equivariant GNNs achieve DFT-level accuracy with million-fold speedups

\textbf{This is not speculative futurism}. Every component—the AI models, the DFT calculations, the acoustic measurements—exists and is validated. The challenge is integration, not invention.
